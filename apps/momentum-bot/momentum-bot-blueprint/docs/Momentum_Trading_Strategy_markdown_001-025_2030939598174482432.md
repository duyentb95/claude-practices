# Momentum and Mean Reversion

There are TWO main Market Regimes that you will see in Price Action. 

# Momentum:

• also known as “trending price action” 

- the price action appears to be “one-directional” 

# Mean Reversion:

- also known as “ranging price action” 

- the price action appears to be “chopping” and “moving sideways”, without a clear direction.

# Best Momentum Conditions

# Some Context:

Trading is a Zero-Sum Game. In order for us to win we need another player to lose. 

This means when we think about the best time to trade Momentum, is the exact same time for when it is the worst time to trade Mean Reversion. 

When the market is blasting in 1 direction like a rocket, all the traders who are betting on a reversal continue to get stopped out and add more fuel to the fire. 

# THE TOP 3 Variables for a Momentum Regime:

Variable #1: “Grindy Staircase” Price Action 

Variable #2: Increasing Volume over Time 

Variable #3: High Volatility 

# Variable #1:

# the “Grindy Staircase” price action

![image](https://cdn-mineru.openxlab.org.cn/result/2026-03-06/640370d7-abae-490b-ab91-b577aeb358d2/68cd0ed65ccec364ffd31dc783a03051009101b9345270762b175bf04a1f8e03.jpg)


The main reason why this is fantastic price action to see for ALL Momentum Trading Strategies is because of the “High Margin of Error”. 

A “High Margin of Error” means you can have an imperfect entry, an imperfect stoploss and an imperfect target and still get away with a winning trade. 

This means that regardless of how exactly you take a trade in this kind of environment, as long as you are doing something that looks like "buying high and selling higher", the market will likely reward you for it regardless of how you actually executed the trade. 

# The Entry

If a Trader tried to get long at ANY of the resistance breaks (green circles), every single one of the Momentum Trade attempts would have worked. 

If they entered early or late...it doesn't matter. The market would reward them anyway. 

Meanwhile every Trader who would have been attempting to "Short Resistances" would continuously fail. 

If they entered early or late... it doesn't matter. The market would still punish them as the market pushed higher in every instance. 

# The Stoploss

Similar logic applies to the Stoploss placement. 

Regardless of how WIDE or TIGHT the stoploss placement is, price barely pulls back during the entire regime so all types of stoploss placements would work. 

Swing low stoplosses, trailing stoplosses, ATR stoplosses.... they ALL would have worked for Traders who were "Buying High and Selling Higher" 

But for Traders trying to short resistance... ALL types of stoplosses would still be hit! 

# The Target

![image](https://cdn-mineru.openxlab.org.cn/result/2026-03-06/640370d7-abae-490b-ab91-b577aeb358d2/04472469055553bd91a61c1d5627892d80270728482956e917f514ba7632c842.jpg)


If a Momentum Trader is playing it safe and going for a small target OR being very ambitious and going for a very big target... it doesn't matter! 

Most variations of trying to take profit on a Momentum Trade would likely result in a win. 

But Mean Reversion Trades who are trying to short would seriously struggle... no matter how small or wide of a Target they use, price doesn't go anywhere near it since it just keeps pushing higher. 

![image](https://cdn-mineru.openxlab.org.cn/result/2026-03-06/640370d7-abae-490b-ab91-b577aeb358d2/ba7b99ca2d0d23bdec7cf72dff772ad87f8a8b84bc6ad021cb0ebf8de0321ba5.jpg)


So when the price action looks like a "Grindy Staircase", this is similar to the weather conditions being like a tornado outside. 

You will get away with most variations/style of entry/stoploss/target, just like how you will get away with flying all types of kites (even a washing machine) during a tornado.

"Okay so a Grindy Staircase is good for Momentum... but how? What's actually happening in the orderbook?" 

# Before I answer this question for you, I want to give you some context first.

- The market doesn't move because of lines drawn on a chart or because of a pattern in the candlesticks. 

- The market moves based on Imbalances in the Supply/Demand. 

- Supply = Limit Orders which are resting in the orderbook. 

- Demand = Market Orders which aggressively execute against the resting limit orders. 

# On the next few pages below, I will demonstrate this with 2 Examples

Market Order Imbalances

So there is an imbalance in the Limit Orders (the limit sells are much thicker than the limit buys) 

Now let’s imagine that every 1 minute that passes, $10 of Market Buys and$ 10 of Market Sells go through. 

Despite there is an EQUAL AMOUNT of Market buys/sells that are coming through, the price will MOVE DOWN. 

This is because the pressure moving in both sides is EQUAL, but the book is THINNER on the way down.

# QUICK TIP:

Volume is just the sum of all the Market Buys + Market Sells that were executed over the previous $N$ units of time. 

The next variable to keep an eye on in a Momentum Regime is the Volume. 

When it is consistently increasing over time this is a sign that the market is “heating up” over time. It shows that more and more attention is moving onto that product and there are more and more players willing to participate in trading that coin. 

When looking to “Buy High and Sell Higher,” ideally we will need to be trading in a market regime that is getting more and more wild after we enter a trade.
